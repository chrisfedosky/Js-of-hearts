Hearts is a "trick-avoidance" game where the goal is to have the
lowest score when a player reaches 100 points.

4-Player Rules (Standard)

The Deal: Each player receives 13 cards from a standard 52-card deck.
Passing: Before play starts, players choose 3 cards to pass to an opponent. The direction rotates: Left (Hand 1), Right (Hand 2), Across (Hand 3), No Pass (Hand 4).
Gameplay:
The player with the 2 of Clubs leads the first trick.
Players must follow the suit led. If they cannot, they may play any card—except a point card (Heart or Queen of Spades) on the very first trick.
The highest card of the suit led wins the trick and leads the next one.
Breaking Hearts: You cannot lead a Heart until one has been "broken" (played as a discard when someone couldn't follow another suit).
Scoring:
Each Heart = 1 point.
Queen of Spades = 13 points.
Shooting the Moon: If you take all 13 Hearts and the Queen of Spades, you get 0 points while everyone else gets 26 (or you subtract 26 from your score).

2-Player Rules (Variations)

Because 52 cards cannot be divided evenly for 2 players while maintaining the same strategy, adjustments are made:

Removed Cards: Often, certain cards (like 3s, 5s, 7s, 9s, Jacks, and Kings) are removed to leave a smaller deck (28 cards), and 13 cards are dealt to each player.
Dummy Hand: Alternatively, use a full deck and deal 16 cards to each player, 16 to a face-up "dummy" hand, and 4 to a face-down "blind" pile.
No Passing: Passing is usually skipped in 2-player versions.
Scoring Adjustments: If cards were removed, "Shooting the Moon" might only be worth 20 points since fewer Hearts are in play.

Shooting the Moon is a high-risk "hail Mary" that can flip the game's leaderboard. To pull it off, you need to collect all

13 Hearts and the Queen of Spades.

1. Identify a "Moon" Hand

Don't attempt it unless your hand meets these criteria:

High Hearts: You ideally need the Ace, King, and Queen of Hearts. Without them, an opponent can easily hold their high heart to "break" your run at the very end.
A "Long Suit": Having 6-7+ cards in a single suit (e.g., Diamonds) is crucial. Once you pull the higher cards of that suit from your opponents, you can "run the table" with your remaining low cards because they have none left to beat you.
Control Cards: High Spades (Ace or King) or the Queen of Spades itself are vital for making sure you capture that 13-point card when it eventually falls.

2. Strategic Passing

The "All-In" Pass: Instead of passing high cards, pass your lowest cards (2s and 3s). This ensures that when you lose control of the lead, you don't accidentally lose a "garbage" trick that contains a point card.
Avoid "Alarm Bells": Passing a low Heart is a massive red flag to experienced players. To be subtle, pass low Diamonds or Clubs instead.

3. The Gameplay Phase

Duck Early, Dominate Late: Play low non-point cards in early tricks to see where the high "stopper" cards (like the Ace of Hearts) are.
Flush the Stoppers: Use your high non-heart cards (like the Ace of Clubs) to take the lead, then play high cards in your "long suit" to force opponents to discard their Hearts.
The "Accidental" Win: If you take the Queen of Spades early, play it off as an accident to keep opponents from realizing you're now hunting for the rest of the Hearts.
Count the Hearts: You must track all 13 Hearts. If you miss even one, you'll end up with a devastating 25-point penalty instead of a 0.

4. Defensive Play (How to Stop a Shooter)

If you suspect someone is shooting the moon:

Take a "Bullet": Intentionally win a trick that has a Heart in it. Even one point prevents them from shooting.
Save a High Heart: Keep the Ace or King of Hearts for the very last tricks to snatch a point away from the shooter.

Beyond the standard rules, "Omnibus Hearts" and other house variations add layers of strategy to the game.

1. The Jack of Diamonds (Omnibus Hearts)

This is the most popular variant.

The Bonus: The Jack of Diamonds (or sometimes the 10 of Diamonds) acts as a "bonus card" worth -10 points.
Strategy: Unlike Hearts or the Queen of Spades, you want to win the trick containing this card.
Shooting the Moon: In some versions, you must capture the Jack of Diamonds along with all Hearts and the Queen of Spades to successfully shoot the moon. In others, it simply adds an extra -10 point bonus to your successful "moon".

2. Spot Hearts

In this version, Hearts are not worth a flat 1 point each.

Face Value Scoring: Each Heart card is worth its numerical value (e.g., 2 of Hearts = 2 points, 10 of Hearts = 10 points).
High Cards: Face cards usually carry higher penalties: Jack = 11, Queen = 12, King = 13, and Ace = 14 (or 15).
Higher Stakes: Because point totals are much higher, the game is often played to 500 points instead of 100. 

3. The 10 of Clubs (The "Multiplier")

Some players include the 10 of Clubs as a special modifier.

Double Points: The player who takes the 10 of Clubs doubles their score for that round. If you took 3 Hearts and the 10 of Clubs, you'd get 6 points instead of 3.
High Risk: This card makes every Heart you take twice as painful, making it a powerful defensive weapon to "dump" on an opponent.

4. Other Common "House Rules"

Shooting the Sun: If a player wins every single trick in a round (not just the point cards), they "Shoot the Sun" for a massive -52 point bonus.
The 100-Point Reset: If a player’s score hits exactly 100, it is reset to 0 (or sometimes 50).
No First Trick Points: A nearly universal rule where no point cards (Hearts or the Queen of Spades) can be played on the very first trick of the hand.
